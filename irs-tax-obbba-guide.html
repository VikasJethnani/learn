CRITICAL IRC SECTIONS FOR US TECH

These are the sections you'll encounter weekly. Know them cold.

Section 174 / New Section 174A — Research & Experimental Expenditures

This is the single most impactful tax section for US tech companies. Before TCJA's 2022 change, companies could immediately expense all R&D costs. Then TCJA forced 5-year amortization of domestic R&E (15 years for foreign). The OBBBA reversed this with new Section 174A.

Period Treatment Impact on Tech
Pre-2022 Immediate expensing of all R&E costs Large current deductions, lower taxable income
2022–2024 5-year amortization (domestic) / 15-year (foreign) Massive tax increases for R&D-heavy tech companies
2025+ (OBBBA) New §174A: full expensing restored for domestic R&E Immediate cash tax savings; DTA/DTL remeasurement
CMA Impact: When your company capitalizes software development costs for GAAP (ASC 350-40) but expenses them for tax under §174A, you create a taxable temporary difference that generates a deferred tax liability. You'll need to model this every quarter.
// Journal Entry: Tax Benefit from R&D Expensing // GAAP: Capitalized $10M software dev costs, amortize over 3 years // Tax: Expense $10M immediately under §174A Year 1 — Taxable temp difference = $10M - $3.33M = $6.67M Dr Income Tax Expense $1,400,700 // 6.67M × 21% Cr Deferred Tax Liability $1,400,700 // This DTL reverses as GAAP amortization catches up to tax Year 2 — DTL reversal = $3.33M × 21% = $700,000 Year 3 — DTL reversal = $3.33M × 21% = $700,000

Section 168(k) — Bonus Depreciation

Allows businesses to deduct the full cost of qualifying assets in the year placed in service rather than depreciating over the asset's useful life. The OBBBA permanently restored 100% bonus depreciation for property acquired after January 19, 2025.

Section 163(j) — Business Interest Expense Limitation

Limits the deduction for business interest expense to 30% of Adjusted Taxable Income (ATI). The OBBBA permanently restored the more generous EBITDA-based ATI calculation, meaning depreciation and amortization are added back.

CMA Impact: If your tech company carries significant debt (acquisition financing, credit facilities), the switch back to EBITDA-based ATI means you can deduct more interest expense. Any previously disallowed interest carried forward as a DTA may now become realizable — triggering a DTA valuation allowance reassessment.

Section 162(m) — Executive Compensation Limitation

Limits the deductibility of compensation paid to "covered employees" to $1 million per person. After TCJA, this covers the CEO, CFO, and the three next highest-paid officers. Starting 2027, it expands to the five next highest-paid employees.

Why This Matters in Tech: Tech companies pay executives primarily in stock-based compensation (RSUs, options). When an RSU vests at $5M, GAAP records the expense over the vesting period, but tax can only deduct up to $1M. The excess $4M is a permanent difference — it increases your effective tax rate (ETR) permanently and never reverses.
// Example: CFO RSU compensation Total comp = $8,000,000 Tax deductible = $1,000,000 // §162(m) limit Permanent difference = $7,000,000 // ETR impact: Additional tax = $7M × 21% = $1,470,000 // This shows up as a permanent item in your rate reconciliation // No DTA or DTL — it's gone forever

💡 Key Insights

  • Section 174A is the #1 section for tech — master the book-tax difference between ASC 350-40 capitalization and tax expensing
  • 100% bonus depreciation creates large DTLs that reverse over the GAAP useful life of assets
  • 163(j) EBITDA-based calculation can unlock previously disallowed interest deductions and affect DTA realizability
  • 162(m) is a permanent difference — no deferred tax, just straight ETR impact from exec comp

🎯 Impact On Your Work

  • You'll compute 174A temporary differences every quarter — build a schedule that tracks GAAP amortization vs tax expensing
  • Bonus depreciation fixed asset schedules need to reconcile with your company's fixed asset sub-ledger
  • During quarterly provision, always check if 163(j) carryforwards need DTA/VA reassessment
  • The 162(m) permanent difference is a common audit focus — maintain a clear covered employee comp schedule

The One, Big, Beautiful Bill Act — Decoded

Signed into law July 4, 2025 as Public Law 119-21. Here's everything that changed, when it takes effect, and what it means for your provision work.

ENACTMENT TIMELINE

Different provisions have different effective dates — get these wrong and your provision is wrong

January 20, 2025 — Bonus Depreciation Retroactive Date

100% bonus depreciation applies to qualified property acquired after this date. If your company bought assets between 1/20 and 7/4, they retroactively qualify for full expensing.

January 1, 2025 — R&D Expensing (§174A) Effective

Domestic R&E costs are immediately deductible for tax years beginning after December 31, 2024. Calendar-year companies: this applies to your full 2025 tax year.

January 1, 2025 — 163(j) EBITDA Restored

The EBITDA-based ATI calculation is permanent for tax years beginning after 12/31/2024. D&A added back to ATI for interest limitation purposes.

July 4, 2025 — Enactment Date (ASC 740 Recognition)

Under ASC 740, effects of the new law are recognized in the period that includes the enactment date. Calendar-year entities recognize all deferred tax remeasurements as a discrete item in Q3 2025.

January 1, 2026 — Estate/Gift Exemption Increase

Unified exemption permanently increases to $15M per individual ($30M married). Annual gift exclusion remains $19,000 for 2026.

January 1, 2026 — 163(j) CFC Exclusion

New limitation: CFC-related items excluded from domestic ATI calculation. Companies with CFCs may see reduced interest deductibility starting 2026.

Deferred Tax Deep Dive — ASC 740 Under the OBBBA

The OBBBA created the most complex deferred tax remeasurement event since TCJA in 2017. Here's exactly how to handle it.

⚖

Temporary Differences

When GAAP and tax treat the same item differently in timing, creating DTAs or DTLs that reverse over time.

⚒

Permanent Differences

Items treated differently by GAAP and tax that never reverse — they permanently affect your ETR.

⚠

Valuation Allowance

The "more likely than not" test: will you actually realize your DTAs? The OBBBA changes the math.

OBBBA-DRIVEN DEFERRED TAX CHANGES

DTL Generators — How R&D and Depreciation Create Deferred Tax Liabilities

When you deduct something for tax before you expense it for GAAP, the tax basis of the asset is lower than the book basis. This creates a taxable temporary difference and a DTL.

Complete DTL Roll-Forward — R&D Example

YearBook BasisTax BasisTemp DiffDTL (@ 21%)DTL Change
Year 1$6.67M$0$6.67M$1,400,700+$1,400,700
Year 2$3.33M$0$3.33M$699,300−$701,400
Year 3$0$0$0$0−$699,300
Key Insight: The DTL is largest in Year 1 when the timing gap is widest. It reverses as GAAP "catches up" through amortization. For a tech company spending $100M+/year on R&D, this creates DTLs in the tens of millions.

DTA Impacts — How the OBBBA Affects Deferred Tax Assets

DTAs represent future tax benefits — amounts you'll save on taxes in future periods. The OBBBA changes which DTAs exist and whether they're realizable.

KEY DTA CATEGORIES AFFECTED

§174 Amortization DTAs

Companies that capitalized R&D for tax in 2022–2024 had DTAs for the unamortized balance. Under OBBBA transition rules, small businesses (≤$25M gross receipts) can deduct these retroactively. Larger companies get a 1-2 year catch-up deduction for remaining balances.

Action: Reverse existing §174 DTAs as they become deductible under OBBBA transition rules. This is a discrete benefit in Q3 2025.

§163(j) Interest Carryforwards

Companies with disallowed business interest from years when EBIT-based ATI was used now have interest carryforward DTAs. The return to EBITDA-based ATI means more current interest is deductible, making carryforwards more likely to be realized.

Action: Reassess VA against 163(j) carryforward DTAs — the broader EBITDA base makes realization more likely.

Stock-Based Compensation DTAs

SBC creates DTAs based on GAAP expense recognized during vesting. At vest/exercise, the DTA is compared to actual tax deduction (FMV at vest). Windfalls (stock up) → excess benefit to APIC. Shortfalls (stock down) → DTA write-off to expense.

OBBBA Note: Expansion of §162(m) covered employees may limit tax deductions for more executives, creating permanent differences that reduce or eliminate the DTA benefit.
// Stock-Based Compensation DTA — RSU Example // Grant: 10,000 RSUs to VP Engineering at grant-date FMV $100/share // 4-year vest, GAAP expense = $250K/year ($1M total) During Vesting Period (each year): Dr Deferred Tax Asset $52,500 // $250K × 21% Cr Income Tax Benefit $52,500 At Vest Date — Stock Price = $150 (Windfall): Tax Deduction = 10,000 × $150 = $1,500,000 DTA balance = $210,000 // $1M × 21% Actual tax benefit = $315,000 // $1.5M × 21% Excess benefit = $105,000 → recognized in income tax expense (ASU 2016-09) At Vest Date — Stock Price = $60 (Shortfall): Tax Deduction = 10,000 × $60 = $600,000 DTA balance = $210,000 Actual tax benefit = $126,000 // $600K × 21% Shortfall = $84,000 → additional tax expense (hits ETR) // BUT if VP is a "covered employee" under §162(m): Tax Deduction CAPPED at $1,000,000 regardless of FMV Actual benefit = $210,000 // $1M × 21% Permanent difference on excess → straight ETR hit

Valuation Allowance Reassessment — The High-Stakes Judgment Call

ASC 740-10-30-5: A valuation allowance shall be recognized for DTAs when it is "more likely than not" (>50%) that some or all of the DTA will not be realized. The OBBBA changes the positive and negative evidence on both sides.

For Entities WITH a Valuation Allowance: The OBBBA can have a significant impact. If OBBBA provisions reduce current taxable income (via R&D expensing and bonus depreciation), this may mean the company needs to increase its VA because there is less current taxable income to absorb DTA benefits. Conversely, if OBBBA creates large DTLs that reverse in periods when DTAs also reverse, those DTLs become a source of future taxable income that supports DTA realization — potentially allowing a VA release.

The Q3 2025 Provision — Step-by-Step Discrete Recognition

ASC 740-10-45-15: The effect of a change in tax law is recognized in the period that includes the enactment date. For calendar-year entities, that's Q3 2025 (July 4 enactment). Here's the workflow:

// ═══ Q3 2025 DISCRETE TAX PROVISION WORKFLOW ═══ STEP 1: Identify All Affected Deferred Tax Balances // Review every line item on your deferred tax roll-forward ├── §174 R&D amortization DTAs (from 2022-2024 capitalization) ├── §168(k) bonus depreciation DTLs ├── §163(j) interest carryforward DTAs ├── NOL carryforward DTAs ├── R&D credit carryforward DTAs ├── SBC-related DTAs (for covered employees under §162(m)) └── International: GILTI/FDII deferred taxes STEP 2: Remeasure Beginning-of-Period DT Balances // Use the NEW law to re-calculate what DTAs/DTLs would be // as if the law had been in effect at the start of the period For each item: New_DT = Temp_Difference × 21% // rate unchanged, but AMOUNTS change Discrete_Adj = New_DT − Old_DT STEP 3: Record Discrete Tax Expense/Benefit // Do NOT spread this through the AETR // This is a discrete, period-specific item Dr Income Tax Expense (or Benefit) $X Cr Deferred Tax Asset/Liability $X STEP 4: Reassess Valuation Allowances // Under new law, re-evaluate "more likely than not" // Changes to VA are ALSO discrete items in Q3 STEP 5: Update AETR Forecast // The OBBBA affects the AETR for the remainder of 2025 // and all future years — update your forecast model STEP 6: Prepare Disclosures // Rate reconciliation: add line item for "Effect of change in tax law" // Significant components of deferred tax: update all affected items // ASU 2023-09 enhanced disclosures may also be required
Common Mistake: Do NOT include the effect of the law change in your annual effective tax rate (AETR) calculation. It must be recognized as a discrete item in the interim period including the enactment date. Including it in the AETR would spread the impact across remaining quarters, which violates ASC 740-270-25.

💡 Key Insights

  • R&D expensing (§174A) + bonus depreciation (§168k) are the two biggest DTL generators for tech companies under OBBBA
  • Pre-existing §174 DTAs from 2022-2024 capitalization need to be reversed under OBBBA transition rules
  • VA assessment is a two-edged sword: OBBBA creates DTLs (positive evidence for DTA realization) but also reduces taxable income (potentially negative evidence)
  • All remeasurements are DISCRETE items in Q3 2025 — never spread through the AETR

🎯 Impact On Your Work

  • Build an "OBBBA Deferred Tax Remeasurement" workpaper with every affected line item, old balance, new balance, and discrete adjustment
  • Work closely with your tax team to model the §174A transition — the catch-up deduction for prior year capitalized R&D is a one-time benefit
  • Document your VA assessment thoroughly — this is the #1 audit focus area in post-OBBBA provisions
  • Your Q3 2025 ETR reconciliation needs a prominent "Effect of OBBBA enactment" line item — prepare the disclosure language early

International Tax & Tech — The GILTI/FDII Overhaul

If your tech company has even one foreign subsidiary, international tax provisions define a huge chunk of your provision. The OBBBA rewrote the rules.

🌐

GILTI → NCTI

Global Intangible Low-Taxed Income becomes Net CFC Tested Income. Not just a name change — the QBAI exemption is eliminated, broadening the tax base.

📦

FDII → FDDEI

Foreign-Derived Intangible Income becomes Foreign-Derived Deduction Eligible Income. The incentive rate rises from 13.125% to 14%, and QBAI is removed.

🛡

BEAT — Modified

Base Erosion Anti-Abuse Tax remains but with expanded covered payments and rate adjustments. Affects companies making large deductible payments to foreign affiliates.

GILTI → Net CFC Tested Income (NCTI) — The Full Picture

GILTI was designed to tax income earned by CFCs above a "routine" return on tangible assets (QBAI). The OBBBA eliminates the QBAI concept entirely, meaning all CFC tested income is subject to US tax.

ElementOld (GILTI)New (NCTI)Impact
Base CFC tested income minus 10% of QBAI All CFC tested income (no QBAI exemption) Broader base — more income taxed
Deduction 50% deduction (§250) 40% deduction (permanent) Effective rate: 10.5% → 12.6%
FTC Limitation 80% of foreign taxes creditable 80% maintained Unchanged but applied to larger base
Country-by-Country Blended basket (all CFCs aggregated) Country-by-country allocation Major — can't blend high/low tax jurisdictions
Deferred Tax Policy Election: deferred or period cost Same election available Companies using deferred approach must remeasure
Critical for Tech: Companies with significant tangible assets overseas (data centers, manufacturing equipment) benefited enormously from the 10% QBAI exemption. Its elimination doubles the effective rate on CFC income in many cases. If your company elected to record deferred taxes on GILTI, you must remeasure all NCTI-related deferred tax balances at the new 12.6% effective rate as a discrete item in Q3 2025.

FDII → FDDEI — The Export Incentive

FDII was designed to incentivize US companies to serve foreign markets from the US (rather than moving IP offshore). The OBBBA renames it FDDEI and makes the rate slightly less favorable but permanent.

This creates a tax incentive structure: best rate for keeping IP domestic and exporting, medium rate for offshore CFC income, highest rate (21%) for purely domestic income.

Common Tech Company IP Structures — Before and After OBBBA

Many US tech companies structured their IP ownership through low-tax jurisdictions (Ireland, Netherlands, Singapore). The OBBBA's elimination of QBAI and move to country-by-country NCTI fundamentally changes the calculus.

Structure A: IP Offshore (Pre-OBBBA)

IP held by Irish subsidiary. US parent licenses IP from CFC. Profits accumulate offshore at low local rate.

Post-OBBBA:

  • QBAI exemption gone — all CFC income taxed at 12.6%
  • Country-by-country means can't blend with high-tax jurisdictions
  • Significantly less beneficial than before
Favored Post-OBBBA

Structure B: IP Domestic (FDDEI)

IP held by US parent. Foreign revenue qualifies for FDDEI deduction. R&D costs fully expensed under §174A.

Post-OBBBA Advantages:

  • 14% effective rate on foreign-derived income
  • Full R&D expensing under §174A (immediate)
  • No transfer pricing risk on IP migration
  • Simpler compliance — no CFC reporting for IP income

Structure C: Hybrid (Cost-Sharing)

R&D performed in US, costs shared with foreign subsidiary via cost-sharing arrangement. IP co-owned.

Post-OBBBA Complications:

  • Foreign R&D still amortized over 15 years (§174A domestic-only)
  • Cost-sharing payments may trigger BEAT
  • NCTI applies to CFC's share of profits at 12.6%
CMA Action Item: If your company is evaluating IP restructuring post-OBBBA, you'll need to model the deferred tax impact of migrating IP back to the US. This involves: (1) potential gain recognition on IP transfer, (2) new FDDEI benefit calculations, (3) unwind of existing NCTI deferred taxes, and (4) transfer pricing documentation. Coordinate with your Big 4 advisors for the transfer pricing study.

💡 Key Insights

  • QBAI elimination is the single biggest international tax change — doubles effective NCTI rate for asset-heavy offshore structures
  • Country-by-country NCTI prevents blending high and low-tax CFC income — each jurisdiction evaluated separately
  • The 12.6% NCTI rate vs 14% FDDEI rate creates a deliberate incentive structure favoring domestic IP with foreign sales
  • Companies with the "deferred" GILTI accounting policy must remeasure all NCTI deferred taxes at Q3 2025 enactment

🎯 Impact On Your Work

  • Review your company's CFC structure and model the NCTI impact at 12.6% without QBAI — this may be a material ETR impact
  • If your company uses the "deferred" approach for GILTI, prepare discrete remeasurement calculations for Q3 2025
  • Coordinate with transfer pricing team on any IP restructuring analysis — the deferred tax consequences are significant
  • Update your international tax provision model: NCTI/FDDEI calculations, FTC limitations, and BEAT applicability testing

In Big 4, you reviewed these. Now you build them. Each workpaper has a specific purpose, update frequency, and audit trail requirement.

1. Deferred Tax Roll-Forward

Frequency: Quarterly
Content: Every temporary difference, beginning balance, current year movement, and ending balance. Split by federal, state, and international.
OBBBA Addition: Add an "OBBBA Remeasurement" column to isolate the discrete Q3 2025 adjustment.
Audit Focus: Completeness of temp differences and rate accuracy.

2. ETR Reconciliation

Frequency: Quarterly
Content: Walk from 21% statutory to actual ETR. Each line item needs a supporting calculation.
OBBBA Addition: "Effect of law change" discrete line item with detailed support memo.
Audit Focus: Accuracy of each rate driver and completeness of permanent items.

3. Valuation Allowance Memo

Frequency: Quarterly assessment, full memo annually
Content: All positive and negative evidence, scheduling of temp diff reversals, sources of taxable income.
OBBBA Addition: Impact of new DTLs as source of future income; effect on DTA scheduling.
Audit Focus: #1 audit area — judgment-heavy, material impact potential.

4. §174A R&D Tracking Schedule

Frequency: Monthly
Content: Domestic vs foreign R&D spend, GAAP capitalization under ASC 350-40, tax expensing under §174A, resulting temporary difference.
Key Reconciliation: R&D spend per GL → GAAP treatment → Tax treatment → Temp diff → DT impact.

5. International Tax Provision

Frequency: Quarterly
Content: NCTI computation by CFC (country-by-country), FDDEI calculation, FTC limitation, BEAT testing if applicable.
OBBBA Update: Rebuild NCTI model without QBAI; apply 40% deduction; new rate of 12.6%.

6. Uncertain Tax Positions (FIN 48)

Frequency: Quarterly review, annual refresh
Content: All tax positions that don't meet "more likely than not" recognition threshold. OBBBA creates new positions (§174A eligibility, FDDEI qualification) that need UTB assessment.

Career Growth Roadmap — CMA in US Tech Tax

Your CMA + Big 4 background gives you a foundation. Here's how to level up in a US tech corporate tax function and what skills differentiate each level.

Year 1-2: Tax Staff / Senior Tax Analyst

Where You Are Now. You're executing the provision workpapers, maintaining the deferred tax roll-forward, preparing R&D credit documentation, and supporting the quarterly close.

Skills to Master: ASC 740 mechanics cold — you should be able to explain any deferred tax balance to an auditor. Learn your company's specific tax profile: which sections drive the ETR, what's the VA position, what are the material UTBs. Build Excel models that scale.

Year 3-5: Tax Manager

The Transition. You move from executing to owning. You own the full provision process, manage external auditor relationships, and start making judgment calls on VA and UTBs.

Skills to Master: International tax provisions (NCTI/FDDEI), state tax apportionment, tax automation tools (OneSource, Corptax, Longview), and stakeholder communication. You should be able to brief the CFO on ETR drivers in 5 minutes.

Year 5-8: Senior Tax Manager / Tax Director

Strategic Influence. You drive tax planning: M&A tax structuring, IP location decisions, credit optimization, and ETR management. You interact with the board's audit committee.

Skills to Master: M&A tax due diligence (§338(h)(10), §382 NOL limitations), transfer pricing strategy, tax controversy management, and tax technology transformation. Your CMA gives you a cost management edge that pure CPAs lack.

Year 8+: VP of Tax / Head of Tax

The Goal. You own the tax function, manage a team of 5-20+, report to the CFO, and present to the audit committee. You set the company's global tax strategy.

Differentiators: Business acumen beyond tax — understand product strategy, market expansion, and how tax enables (or constrains) business decisions. Pillar 2 (global minimum tax) compliance, ESG tax reporting, and tax technology investment decisions become your domain.
Your CMA Advantage: Unlike CPAs who come from an audit/assurance mindset, your CMA certification emphasizes management accounting — strategic planning, performance management, and decision analysis. In a corporate tax function, this translates to a natural ability to connect tax outcomes to business decisions. When the VP of Tax needs someone to model "what happens to our ETR if we acquire this company," your CMA training in financial analysis makes you the person they turn to.

Section 174 — The Full History

Section 174 has been one of the most volatile sections in the IRC:

  • 1954-2021: Taxpayers could immediately deduct R&E expenditures or elect to capitalize and amortize over 60+ months
  • 2017 TCJA: Amended §174 to require capitalization and amortization over 5 years (domestic) or 15 years (foreign), effective for tax years beginning after 12/31/2021
  • 2022-2024: Mandatory amortization in effect. Tech companies saw massive tax increases as billions in R&D costs could no longer be immediately deducted
  • 2025 OBBBA: New §174A restores immediate expensing for domestic R&E. Foreign R&E remains subject to 15-year amortization. Small businesses (≤$25M gross receipts) can retroactively expense back to 2022

Why This Matters: For a tech company spending $500M+ annually on R&D, the difference between immediate expensing and 5-year amortization is a ~$400M temporary difference, translating to ~$84M in deferred tax impact. This single section can move your ETR by 2-4 percentage points.

Bonus Depreciation — How It Works

What Qualifies: Tangible property with a recovery period of 20 years or less (MACRS), certain computer software, and qualified improvement property. The property must be "new to the taxpayer" (used property now qualifies under TCJA).

Interaction with GAAP: Under GAAP, these same assets are depreciated over their useful life (typically 3-10 years for tech equipment). This creates a taxable temporary difference: tax deducts faster than GAAP, so the tax basis is lower than the book basis, generating a DTL.

New §168(n): The OBBBA also created a NEW section for "qualified production property" (factories, manufacturing facilities) with 100% expensing through 2032. This is separate from §168(k) and has its own rules for what qualifies.

Section 163(j) — Interest Limitation Details

The Formula: Deductible business interest = Business Interest Income + 30% of ATI + Floor Plan Financing Interest

EBIT vs EBITDA: The critical difference is whether depreciation and amortization are added back to taxable income when computing ATI. With EBITDA (post-OBBBA), ATI is larger, so 30% of ATI is larger, meaning more interest is deductible.

Carryforward: Disallowed interest carries forward indefinitely. These carryforwards create DTAs. The OBBBA's return to EBITDA may make these DTAs more realizable.

2026 Wrinkle: Starting in 2026, CFC-related items are excluded from domestic ATI. If your company's ATI includes significant CFC income, this reduces the interest limitation capacity. Model this carefully for 2026+ forecasts.

ASC 740 — Income Tax Accounting Fundamentals

Core Principle: ASC 740 requires companies to recognize the current and future tax consequences of all events that have been recognized in the financial statements.

Current Tax: Taxes payable (or refundable) for the current year based on taxable income and enacted tax rates.

Deferred Tax: The future tax consequences of temporary differences between the tax basis and book basis of assets and liabilities.

  • DTA: Future tax benefit — tax basis > book basis (you'll deduct more in the future)
  • DTL: Future tax cost — book basis > tax basis (you've already deducted more for tax)
  • Valuation Allowance: Contra-asset reducing DTA when realization is not "more likely than not" (>50%)

Law Change Recognition: ASC 740-10-25-47: Effects of changes in tax laws are recognized in the period that includes the enactment date. This is why the OBBBA (enacted July 4, 2025) creates a discrete item in Q3 2025.
